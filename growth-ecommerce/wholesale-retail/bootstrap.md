# Wholesale / Retail Expansion Program — Bootstrap

> **Inherits from:** `~/.claude/templates/session-kickoff-bootstrap.md`
>
> **Use when:** entering wholesale / retail channels — specialty, mass, club, department store, chain, drug, convenience — from a DTC or Amazon-first starting point.

---

## Who this is for

Brand leaders, VPs of sales, directors of retail at DTC / CPG / consumer brands adding wholesale channels. Programs span 12-36 weeks depending on scope — retail selling cycles are long.

## Opinionated defaults

### Retail channel types

- **Specialty** (local stores, independents): highest margin, lowest volume, best for launch + brand building
- **Mass** (Target, Walmart): high volume, MAP-controlled, national reach
- **Club** (Costco, BJ's, Sam's): extreme volume, compressed margins, SKU specificity
- **Department / chain** (Nordstrom, Macy's, Dick's): curated assortment, elevated positioning
- **Drug / convenience** (CVS, Walgreens, 7-Eleven): impulse / replenishment, fast turnover expectation
- **Omni-channel platforms** (Amazon, Target+, Walmart Connect): marketplace-adjacent but act as channels

### Retail buyer process

- **Pitch → pilot → scale → national:** typically 6-24 months from first conversation to national rollout
- **Category reviews:** annual or biannual; miss the window = wait a year
- **Retailer product-development calendar:** plan 12-18 months out
- **In-person matters** at most retailers (Target, Whole Foods, REI, Dick's) — Zoom for pilot, in-person for scale

### Margin structure

- **Keystone pricing:** 50% margin wholesale-to-retail (standard)
- **Actual spread:** 35-55% depending on category + channel
- **Trade spend:** 10-20% of wholesale revenue typical (co-op, slotting, promos, demos)
- **Chargebacks:** 5-15% of wholesale revenue (returns, damages, short-ships, compliance fees)
- **Net margin to brand:** 15-30% of retail price after all deductions for most consumer categories

### MAP + channel conflict

- **MAP policy required** before entering multi-channel wholesale. Without it, Amazon discounters destroy retail partner relationships.
- **Enforce MAP aggressively** — the policy is only as strong as enforcement
- **DTC pricing strategy:** equal-to-MAP (preserves partners) or premium-to-MAP (partners offer discount, DTC captures direct margin)
- **Channel-exclusive SKUs** preserve retail relationships while allowing DTC flexibility

### Planogram + merchandising

- **Primary driver of retail velocity:** shelf location, facings, placement
- **Store-level compliance audit** required — sign-off on placement
- **Planogram changes lag reality by months** — don't assume your placement is current
- **Shelf-back strategy:** packaging designed for shelf context (dieline, colorway, hierarchy)

### Co-op + slotting

- **Slotting fees:** one-time payment for shelf space in some channels ($500-$50K per SKU per location). Category-dependent.
- **Co-op marketing:** ongoing promo support; typically 3-8% of wholesale revenue
- **Demo programs:** in-store sampling for velocity launch; high ROI if product is trial-driven

### Broker / rep model

- **Broker:** 5-8% commission; lower cost but less aligned with brand
- **In-house sales rep:** higher cost but owned relationship
- **Hybrid:** broker for small accounts, in-house for strategic accounts
- **Manufacturer's rep organization (MRO):** industry-specific; category-dependent fit

## Step 0 — Interview question additions

1. **Target channels** — which retailers, in what sequence?
2. **Current retail presence** — any existing wholesale relationships?
3. **DTC / Amazon revenue mix** — how dependent on current channel economics?
4. **Product-category fit** — is the product shelf-stable, display-ready, impulse-friendly?
5. **Inventory capacity** — can you support retail orders without starving DTC?
6. **Pricing strategy** — MAP policy exists? Enforceable?
7. **Packaging retail-ready?** — dieline, barcoding, case pack, pallet spec
8. **Compliance infrastructure** — EDI, GS1 barcodes, test-and-hold requirements, retail-specific labels
9. **Sales team / broker relationships** — in-house, broker, MRO, none?
10. **Trade-spend budget** — capacity for co-op + slotting + demos
11. **Freight / logistics** — shipping to retail DCs (distribution centers) vs. store-direct
12. **Planning horizon** — target retailers have calendars; which windows are you hitting?

## Step 2 — Milestone template (24-week program)

```markdown
### M1 — Channel Strategy + Target List (week 1-3)
- Deliverable: prioritized retailer list; entry-sequence plan; pricing strategy
- Validation: MAP policy drafted; channel conflict mapped
- Target: end of week 3

### M2 — Retail Readiness (week 3-8)
- Deliverable: packaging retail-ready; compliance infrastructure (EDI, GS1); case-pack + pallet spec
- Validation: packaging passes retail requirements; compliance systems operational
- Target: end of week 8

### M3 — Buyer Pitch Wave (week 6-16)
- Deliverable: pitches delivered to target buyers; pitch deck, samples, category review
- Validation: pitches landed; follow-ups scheduled
- Target: end of week 16

### M4 — Pilot Program (week 12-20)
- Deliverable: pilot orders shipped; in-store placement confirmed
- Validation: product on shelf; sell-through tracking live
- Target: end of week 20

### M5 — Trade Spend + Co-op Activation (week 16-22)
- Deliverable: demo programs, co-op marketing, promo activation
- Validation: programs running; velocity improving
- Target: end of week 22

### M6 — Scale Decision + Expansion (week 22-24)
- Deliverable: sell-through review; expansion recommendations; reorder planning
- Validation: data-driven kill / scale / expand decision per retailer
- Target: end of week 24
```

## Step 3 — Critical mental model

> **Wholesale is not DTC with middlemen.**
>
> In DTC, you own the customer relationship and optimize for LTV. In wholesale, the retailer owns the customer relationship and optimizes for sell-through on their shelf. These are different businesses with different unit economics, timelines, and operational requirements.
>
> The #1 mistake is thinking wholesale will be "like DTC but at scale." Wholesale requires: retail-ready packaging, EDI compliance, trade spend, planogram management, buyer relationships, retailer-calendar planning, MAP enforcement, chargeback management, and a sales motion with 6-24 month cycles.
>
> Budget for this or don't enter wholesale. The brands that succeed have a dedicated retail team with retail-experienced leadership. The brands that fail try to bolt wholesale onto an ecommerce team with no retail DNA.

## Step 4 — Domain validators

- **MAP policy enforceable:** violations detectable + remediable within 14 days
- **Packaging retail-ready:** dieline, barcodes, case pack, pallet spec, claims compliant
- **EDI operational:** orders, invoices, ASNs flow cleanly
- **Chargeback tracking:** categorized by cause (returns, damages, compliance, short-ship, etc.)
- **Sell-through data flowing:** retailer POS data ingested weekly or monthly

## Step 8 — Working protocol additions

- **Never enter wholesale without MAP policy + enforcement capacity**
- **Never under-estimate chargebacks** — budget 5-15% of wholesale revenue
- **Never miss a retailer calendar window** — wait a year for next one
- **Never over-commit inventory** to DTC when retail order is imminent
- **Never hide trade spend** — full cost of wholesale includes it
- **Always track sell-through, not just sell-in** — sitting on shelf is bad
- **Always over-communicate with buyers** — retail relationships are personal
